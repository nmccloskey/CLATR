Neurolinguistics and psycholinguistics are closely related fields within the study of language, but they focus on different aspects of how language is processed and understood. Neurolinguistics primarily investigates the relationship between language and brain function. Researchers in this field seek to identify which areas of the brain are involved in language comprehension, production, and development. Using brain imaging technologies such as fMRI and EEG, neurolinguists map out the neural pathways activated during language tasks. A key interest is understanding how brain damage, as in aphasia, affects linguistic abilities, and how various brain regions contribute to specific language functions like syntax or phonology.

In contrast, psycholinguistics focuses more broadly on the mental processes involved in acquiring, understanding, and producing language, often without directly referencing the brain’s anatomical structures. Psycholinguists study how language is represented in the mind and how real-time processes like word recognition and sentence parsing unfold. Their research often involves behavioral experiments that measure reaction times, accuracy, or error patterns to infer how cognitive systems handle language. For example, psycholinguists investigate how children learn language, how adults process complex sentences, and how bilinguals manage two languages.

While both fields explore aspects of language processing, neurolinguistics is more concerned with the biological basis of these processes, whereas psycholinguistics examines cognitive functions and representations. Neurolinguistics might ask where in the brain a process occurs, while psycholinguistics is more focused on how the process works. The two fields complement each other, offering different perspectives on the same fundamental questions about language.
